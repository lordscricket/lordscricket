Rules

Here are the 7 rules and 1 principle of Lords Cricket:

  1. Teams: Two Teams of (ideally) 5 players. There can be as few as 4 on either side and as many as 6. Before the game starts, the captains should swap players between teams if necessary to ensure as close a match as possible.
  2. Format: We usually follow a 25 overs per side format that can be played in 3 hours. The captains agree at the start of the game as to the maximum number of overs per side, and accordingly the maximum allowed to any one bowler. (Usually for 25-over matches it will be 7, 6, or 5 for teams of 4, 5 or 6 players respectively.)
  3. Fielders: The batting team lends the fielding team any needed reserve fielders ‚Äì keeping the number of fielders (including bowler and wicket-keeper) at 7 if possible at all times. Obviously the extra fielder/s must genuinely try‚Ä¶ ‚Ä¶to make fielding mistakes look genuine!
  4. Batting order: Each team bats down to number 11 as per real cricket, and the innings is over when 10 wickets have fallen. Each player bats once (in positions 1 to 5 with 5 players), and then players have a second (and sometimes 3rd) bat; in each case the new batter is the player who (a) has had the least number of previous bats, and (b) has scored the least number of runs so far. A player making a duck in their first innings is likely to get a second and possibly third hit later on.
    Retirements: A Batter may choose to retire, but it counts as a wicket on the scoresheet. There‚Äôs no returning and resuming the innings. However, for batting average statistics, it counts as a ‚Äònot out‚Äô.
  5. Batting down the order: When a player bats for a second time (and subsequently), as a handicap, the ‚Äúone-hand-one-bounce-is-half-out‚Äù rule applies. If the batter is caught one-hand-one-bounce (except when the bounce is on the hard pitch surface), it is noted that they are ‚Äúhalf-out‚Äù. The batter can still score as usual on that delivery, and the runs count. Nothing here is credited to the bowler or catcher. If this happens a second time to that batter in that particular innings, however, they are fully out, and in this instance the bowler and catcher receive full (statistical) credit for the wicket.
  6. LBW: No player can be out LBW so long as they were (a) attempting to hit the ball and (b) not taking their batting stance so as to cover the stumps. A player taking guard so as to cover the stumps should be warned by the bowler beforehand.
  7. Extras: A ball that passes the batter above shoulder height, or above waist height on the full, is a no-ball. Any ball which bounces off the pitch laterally is a wide, even if it returns to the pitch. One leg-side delivery is allowed per over, subsequent ones are wides. As in real cricket, no-balls and wides count as 1 extra and must be re-bowled.

Umpiring: Lord‚Äôs cricket has a long tradition of not requiring umpires, instead being based entirely on honour. That is, batters walk when they think they are out, bowlers or the fielding team concede wides / no-balls etc. The bowling team may appeal a wicket to the batter and batting team, and the batters may question whether or not a ball was a wide or no-ball ‚Äì but a wicket or wide not conceded by the opposing team should not be disputed. The captains have an important part to play in fostering nobility and good humour and friendship between all players.

Official Lords Cricket Games
For the purposes of consistency of global statistics, there are a few extra requirements for a game to qualify as a Lords Cricket match:

  1. It must be played with an ordinary tennis ball and ordinary or plastic cricket bats. If possible, the stumps should be raised by ~120mm (e.g. stood on bricks) to allow for the extra bounce.
  2. Boundaries must be at least 30m from the closest stumps. (Where a section of boundary isn‚Äôt, local rules may compensate by, for instance, awarding 2 less runs per boundary scored in that section.)
